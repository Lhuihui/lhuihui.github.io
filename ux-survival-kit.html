Interact with Product Manager
One of the biggest challengs in my work at Watson Developer Platform is trying to refine product manager requirements and increase UX qualify.
There are some practices I found very useful when communicating with product manager:
  • When product manager comes to design with a solution in mind, try to spend time together to understand the original mission and align all stakeholders with the redefined problem statement.
  • When showing design proposals, try to use storyboards to let stakeholders get a grip on target groups, context, product use and timing to understand experience.
  • When the project is in a tight schedule, try to design with developers who will be working on this to get the feasibility checkmark to release the product manager's fears toward jeopardizing releasing date. This will also help you get engineer side's support to push your proposal.
    • Interact with Engineers
  • When gaining a technical understanding of how things work, try to reconfirm your understanding by drawing a diagram or visualizing the relationship in certain way.
  • When designing solutions, try to take valuable input from them since they are the people who understand the system the most, and bring their ideas alive in a user friendly way, which will make them excited and attached to the project.
  • When shipping the product, try to deliver the style and interaction guide in code to help them accelerate development.
    • Interact with Peers
  • When talking about projects, use "we", not "I".
  • When asking for a critique, try to respect other people's time and be very specific about which part you want to get feedback.
  • When giving a critique, try to phrase suggestion in an actionable way.

    IBM has a very strong consulting culture in the organization. We have some good practices on conducting workshops in the context of roadmapping a product or meeting clients.
    Hopes and Fears
    When starting a project, kicking-off a workshop, or bringing in new team members, hopes and fears exericise helps me get to know my teammates, expose aspirations and concerns, and prepare everyone to start.
    Stakeholder Map
    When integrating new team members, starting a new project, exploring a new market, or expanding an offering, this activity helps me identify project stakeholders, their expectations, and relationships.
    Empathy Map
    Empathy Maps help to rapidly put my team in the user's shoes and align on pains and gains - whether at the beginning of a project or mid-stream when we need to re-focus on our users.
    Scenario Map (As-is/To-be)
    As-is Scenario Maps help to document collective understanding of user workflows and are best used as precursors to exploring new ideas. To-be Scenario Maps tell the story of a better experience for our user.
    Big Idea Vignettes
    Once the team has a clear and validated understanding of our user's problems and challenges, this activity is a great way for many people to rapidly brainstorm a breadth of possible ideas.
    Prioritization Grid
    When many items(such as ideas, hills, scenarios, or user stories) are being considered, this activity helps the team evaluate the prioritize them by focusing discussions on importance and feasibility.
    Needs Statements
    This is a very effective activity to use with the team when I feel that we're drifting away from the actual needs, desires, and goals of the user. It helps reorient or reframe the work around the user.
    Storyboarding
    Storyboarding is a way to iterate and communicate ideas and scenarios visually by telling usr-centric stories. If we're having a difficul time just talking about an idea, try some storyboards.
    Assumptions and Questions
    Any time I feel that our team's work needs a "reality check," use this activity to identify and prioritize what assumptions are being made, what we've been guessing about, and what the team still doesn't know.
    Feedback Grid
    This activity helps to gather and organize any sort of feedback and to then unpack questions and ideas - either in real time or after-the-fact - as an efficient means of determining next steps.
    Experience-Based Roadmap
    This activity helps us define a "minimum delightful experience" by scoping big, visionary ideas into more achievable near-term outcomes - while still focusing on the user experience.
    My Passion
    I like in person interactions with people from different backgrounds. I'm learning to be a facilitator between business, design and technology and finding the sweet spot in product design, balancing the business matrix and user need within technology capacity.
